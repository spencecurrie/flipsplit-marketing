A lowball is an attempt to purchase your home at significantly lower than its market value or list price. ## What Is a Lowball Offer?

How low is a lowball? Real estate experts differ on what is considered a lowball offer.

Many would say that a bid’s not a lowball until it’s 20-30% under asking. In general, however, conventional real estate wisdom says any offer lower than 90% of the list price can be considered a lowball.1

#### Why You Need to Identify Lowball Offers

Putting a property on the market can be stressful for any seller—especially if you sell your home conventionally. When bids on your sale come in much lower than expected, you may

-   Second-guess your research
-   Question the skills of any real estate agents you’ve worked with
-   Doubt your ability to recoup your investment in your property
-   Be unable to purchase a new home with the proceeds of the sale

With one or more lowball offers in hand, you may feel forced to:

-   Invest your time in negotiating with the lowball buyers
-   Lower your list price and lose money
-   Further reduce your listing based on market perception from a price drop

One of the most common sources of lowball offers is investors looking to flip a house and capture as much profit as possible, but lowball offers can also come from:

-   Poor guidance from a real estate agent
-   Unfamiliarity with generally accepted real estate negotiation boundaries
-   Cultural differences in bargaining tactics

Even still, the source of lowball offers isn’t all that important. What’s more relevant is knowing how to pick them out and how to firmly say “No.”

## The Strategic Considerations of Lowball Offers

Recognizing when a lowball offer might be strategically used can play a crucial role in achieving a favorable negotiation outcome in real estate transactions. In a market bias favoring buyers, where property listings outpace the pool of interested buyers, such offers might gain acceptance. Optimal times for proposing a lowball offer often coincide with properties lingering unsold or sellers keen on expedited transactions due to urgent circumstances like cash shortages or relocations. Take, for example, a seller motivated by a job transfer. They might display more openness to a negotiation involving a lower price. Still, for an offer to be positively regarded, it must remain within the realms of perception as reasonable and align with both the listing price and market value.

## Strengthening the Proposal of a Lowball Offer

For buyers weighing the possibility of submitting a lowball offer, amplifying the proposal’s attractiveness involves outlining benefits apparent to the seller. This could include adaptable closing terms or demonstrating a genuine negotiating interest on items such as financing or showcasing financial readiness to signal earnest buyer intentions. Furthermore, presenting supportive market comparables that validate the offer figure can enhance its credibility. In scenarios where a property demands substantial repairs, documenting these imperfections and incorporating them into the offer can rationalize the reduced price, aligning with practical pricing models.

## Frequently Asked Questions

#### How does the current real estate market impact whether an offer is considered a lowball?

The state of the market—whether it’s a buyer market or a seller’s market—can greatly influence what’s perceived as a lowball offer. In a buyer market, where inventory is high and demand is lower, buyers may feel more confident submitting lower offers. However, even in these conditions, it’s important to compare against recent listing data to determine what’s truly reasonable. Always assess offers in context with current market trends and your property’s condition.

#### What should I do if I receive multiple offers, but most feel low?

If you’re getting several offers below expectations, first reevaluate your listing price against recent sales and home comps. If your pricing is competitive, multiple low offers could indicate buyers are testing your flexibility. Stay grounded in your data, and consider submitting a firm but fair counter. You might also want to consult with an investment advisor to discuss repositioning or staging strategies that better align with your goals.

#### Can I make a counteroffer even if the original bid feels like a lowball?

Absolutely. A low offer doesn’t mean negotiations are over—it can be the beginning of a deal if handled strategically. Crafting a smart counter offer that reflects your property’s value, recent updates, or desirable location may motivate serious buyers to meet you closer to your expectations. It’s also a way to weed out less committed parties while demonstrating your willingness to engage in good faith negotiation.
